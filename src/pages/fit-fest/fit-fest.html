Gross Motor skills involve the large muscles of the body that enable major body movement such as walking, maintaining balance, coordination, jumping and reaching. They depend on both muscle tone and strength. Children with better-developed motor skills may find it easier to be active and engage in more physical activity than those with less-developed motor skills. The relationship between motor skill performance and physical activity may be important to the health of children, particularly in obesity prevention.

The test comprised two subtests for gross motor development - Locomotor and Object Control - each of which have six skills that assess a different aspect of gross motor development.
* Locomotor skills - those that require fluid movements of the body as the child moves in one direction or the other - namely Run, Gallop, Hop, Leap, Horizontal Jump and Slide
* Object control skills - those that demonstrate efficient throwing, striking, and catching movements - namely Striking a ball, Dribble, Catch, Kick, Overhand throw and underhand roll

These test results are merely observations, and not diagnosis. They specify a performance level at a given time under a particular situation, but they do not tell us why a person performed as he or she did. The primary use of this test is to assess the progress of your child?s gross motor skill development and identify areas to work on.
